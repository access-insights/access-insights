# Phase 1 Strategy Inventory

Last updated: 2026-06-13

## Purpose

This inventory compares the current production and staging website messages, summarizes the relevant research-platform strategy, and identifies implications for the expanded Access Insights sitemap.

Important positioning constraint:

- The new site should be attractive to philanthropic and mission-aligned funders without explicitly asking for funding.
- It should signal an investable infrastructure model through credibility, scale, governance maturity, community value, research operations, and project momentum.
- It should also make Access Insights clearly available for bespoke paid research, Living Labs, pilots, surveys, and advisory work for corporate, nonprofit, academic, and public-sector customers.
- Access Insights' preference is to manage a pool of aligned capital and choose research priorities internally, while still supporting paid customer-directed research when mission-aligned.

## Branch Messaging Comparison

### Production: `main`

What works:

- Strong mission-centered headline: "Innovation shaped by lived experience."
- Clear resonance with disability, accessibility, inclusive UX, and community audiences.
- Positions people with disabilities and older adults as collaborators from the beginning.
- Explains Living Labs as real-world research in authentic community environments.
- Uses proof-oriented market statistics and team credentials.
- Makes the team feel unusually credible and experienced.

Limitations:

- Reads mostly as a high-quality inclusive research consultancy.
- Does not surface the research platform as operating infrastructure.
- Does not make participant program, partner network, articles, current projects, or Proto-Lab feel like durable site destinations.
- Does not explain how community, partner network, research operations, and intelligence compound over time.
- Does not clearly express the implicit funder case for infrastructure capacity.

### Staging: `staging`

What works:

- Stronger corporate-client framing: "Technology is advancing faster than ever. Human adoption is not."
- Clear product and market outcomes: unmet needs, validation, adoption, trust, product-market fit, and better products.
- Introduces the research platform, participant network, Living Labs, and structured innovation pipeline.
- More clearly describes services: opportunity discovery, prototyping, validation, pilots, and scale.
- Better supports bespoke paid research and customer-directed engagements.

Limitations:

- Reduces some of the emotional and community resonance of the production site.
- Risks centering corporate buyers too heavily.
- Does not fully explain participant and partner programs as first-class infrastructure.
- Does not show current projects or ecosystem-building work.
- Does not yet support articles/insights or Proto-Lab.
- Does not clearly distinguish pooled mission-aligned capital from customer-specific sponsored research.

## Core Strategic Thesis

Access Insights is building research innovation infrastructure.

That means creating the trusted systems, participant community, partner network, research workflows, consent practices, data governance, and knowledge layer needed to make human-centered research more repeatable, credible, and scalable.

The site should carry three linked ideas:

- Technology is changing faster than human bodies, senses, cognition, trust, attention, environments, and support needs change.
- Research that intentionally values disability, accessibility, aging, caregiving, context, culture, and human variability reveals where products, services, and systems fail, often producing insights that generalize into better design for everyone.
- Access Insights turns lived experience into structured, ethical, repeatable research operations without selling participant data.

## Audience Hierarchy

Primary strategic audiences:

- Mission-aligned foundations and philanthropic funders.
- Accessibility, disability, aging, independent living, inclusive AI, and civic innovation funders.
- Strategic partners who can contribute sites, referrals, expertise, distribution, capital, or project opportunities.

Primary program audiences:

- People with disabilities.
- Aging adults.
- Caregivers, family members, support professionals, and people whose lived context reveals access barriers.
- Community organizations and partner organizations.

Primary commercial audiences:

- Corporate product, UX, research, AI, accessibility, and innovation teams.
- Product teams needing bespoke research, Living Labs, pilots, surveys, or accessibility-centered validation.
- Startups, public-sector groups, academic teams, and nonprofits with mission-aligned research needs.

Secondary audiences:

- Journalists, conference organizers, ecosystem allies, open source contributors, advisors, and future collaborators.

## Message Pillars

### 1. Infrastructure, Not One-Off Research

Access Insights should be framed as an operating system for repeatable disability-centered product learning, not only a consulting team or participant recruiting pool.

Support points:

- Participant profiles and research readiness reduce repeated intake.
- Partner onboarding and referrals create trusted growth loops.
- Consent, privacy, retention, deletion, and auditability are part of the value proposition.
- Research pipeline workflows help decide which opportunities deserve attention.
- Reusable instruments, question banks, artifact templates, and knowledge-layer concepts turn individual projects into cumulative learning.

### 2. Human Reality In A Fast-Moving Technology Market

The staging headline direction is strategically strong and should influence the expanded site.

Useful framing:

- Technology is moving quickly, but humans are not.
- Products fail when they do not fit real bodies, routines, support systems, environments, and trust patterns.
- Access Insights helps organizations move quickly without losing contact with the people who must live with the outcomes.

### 3. Lived Experience As Innovation Input

The production site's strongest mission language should remain central.

Useful framing:

- People with disabilities and older adults are not edge cases to accommodate after launch.
- Their experience reveals friction, safety risks, adoption barriers, unmet needs, and market opportunities earlier than conventional testing.
- Better accessibility research often produces clearer, safer, more resilient products for broader markets.

### 4. Trust, Consent, And Data Governance

Trust is a business asset and a community commitment.

Important boundaries:

- Access Insights does not sell participant data.
- The value comes from ethical engagement, longitudinal readiness, aggregate insight, de-identified intelligence, and better research operations.
- Privacy, consent, accessibility, and participant dignity should be visible as infrastructure strengths.

### 5. Participants And Partners As Programs

Participant and partner paths should be first-class, not secondary contact options.

Participant value:

- Clear program identity.
- Research readiness profile.
- Communication and accessibility preferences.
- Consent visibility.
- Contribution opportunities through surveys, flash surveys, sessions, referrals, and future advisory paths.
- Support and privacy request paths.

Partner value:

- Structured onboarding.
- Referral pathways.
- Partner-visible opportunities.
- Future Access Stats and community insight access.
- Opportunities to contribute sites, expertise, services, distribution, funding alignment, or project support.

### 6. Bespoke Research Is Available

The site should clearly support customer-directed research without letting that become the only business model.

Offerings to preserve or develop:

- Living Labs and in-setting pilots.
- Opportunity discovery.
- Lived experience advisory.
- Co-design and usability sprints.
- Sponsored community surveys and flash surveys.
- Research and validation.
- Prototyping and solution development.
- Conference-based research activations.
- Advisory and strategy services.

### 7. Current Projects Show Momentum

The site needs a project showcase because funder attractiveness and client confidence both improve when visitors can see active work.

Initial project candidates from personal context:

- Living Labs Independent Living Pilot.
- Virtual Goalball.
- APH partnership exploration connected to virtual goalball education.
- Conference-based research model for CES or DisabilityNext.
- Research platform itself.

Content risk:

- Public details, partner names, status, and claims need approval before publishing.

### 8. Proto-Lab Extends The Mission

Proto-Lab should be a home for Access Insights-originated internal projects that can become open source community assets.

Initial candidates:

- UnisonOS.
- Virtual Lex.
- Potential future accessibility, AI, research, or community tools.

Positioning guidance:

- Present Proto-Lab as an applied experimentation space.
- Avoid over-promising a nonprofit structure before it exists.
- Make it clear that projects may be open source, community-serving, or exploratory.
- Keep it connected to Access Insights' infrastructure story: tools developed through real-world accessibility and research needs can return value to the ecosystem.

### 9. Articles And Insights

The site should include an Articles & Insights destination even before Substack exists.

Recommended approach:

- Start with a static articles index or placeholder content hub.
- Design article cards for title, summary, date, topic, and source.
- Later, link to Substack posts or ingest Substack RSS if desired.
- Keep the site language platform-owned: "Articles & Insights," not "Substack."

## Funder-Attractive Without Direct Funding Ask

Do not use primary CTAs like:

- Fund us.
- Donate.
- Fund the platform.
- Give now.

Prefer language such as:

- Explore strategic collaboration.
- Partner with Access Insights.
- Advance human-centered research innovation.
- Build the infrastructure for better research.
- Collaborate on high-impact research priorities.
- Support scalable, ethical research capacity.

The implicit funder case should come through:

- Infrastructure language.
- Programmatic scale.
- Participant and partner growth loops.
- Governance, consent, privacy, and auditability.
- Current projects and proof points.
- Ecosystem outcomes.
- Clear research priority pipeline.
- Ability to produce measurable community and market insight.

Primary paths:

- Explore current projects.
- Explore Proto-Lab.
- Read Articles & Insights.
- Start a research conversation.

Potential funder path:

- "Explore strategic collaboration" or "Discuss a mission-aligned partnership."

Potential bespoke customer path:

- "Start a research engagement" or "Plan a research project."

Participant and partner platform URLs to confirm:

## Research Platform Value Map For Website

Platform layers to translate into public-facing content:

- Community infrastructure: participant community, referrals, contribution history, research readiness.
- Partner infrastructure: organizations, sites, referrals, partner levels, project opportunities, Access Stats.
- Operational infrastructure: registration, onboarding, consent, communications, surveys, research operations, reporting, support.
- Governance infrastructure: privacy requests, deletion workflows, role-based access, auditability, environment separation.
- Pipeline infrastructure: signal capture, strategic fit, community validation, partner alignment, project scoping, ready-for-research status.
- Knowledge infrastructure: reusable instruments, question banks, templates, de-identified insight products, future intelligence layer.

## Content And Claim Risks

- Do not imply participant data is sold or available to partners.
- Do not overstate current platform features that are still roadmap or staging-only.
- Do not publish private partner names, participant details, sensitive operational details, or funding strategy without approval.
- Validate statistics before reusing the production site's 1.3B, 16%, $13T, and 70% claims.
- Clarify which current projects can be named publicly.
- Confirm participant and partner program public routes before adding CTAs.
- Confirm whether Virtual Lex is the correct name and public framing.
- Avoid direct funding-solicitation language unless the strategy changes.
