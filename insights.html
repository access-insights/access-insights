Research note

Virtual Goalball and binaural audio.

Goalball is an audio-first sport. Players track a moving ball by listening to bells inside the ball, the direction and speed of the throw, the sound of the floor, teammate movement, and referee calls. That makes goalball a powerful research context for studying how binaural audio can support spatial understanding, immersive play, training, and audience experience.

Research intent

Virtual Goalball explores whether recorded and rendered binaural sound can preserve enough spatial detail for people to identify ball paths, react earlier, and experience the sport with a stronger sense of presence.

Research approach

The work starts with calibrated binaural recordings of throws, referee calls, and gameplay. Those recordings create a ground-truth library for comparing listener responses across ball paths, throw types, listening positions, and playback setups.

Solution space

The core solution space includes immersive listening experiences, interactive defensive play, source-localization training, and synthetic binaural rendering that can simulate moving ball trajectories beyond the original recordings.

Key research questions.

The research focuses on what people can understand from sound alone, how quickly they can act on that information, and which audio techniques create the most useful experience.

  • Can listeners accurately identify the starting point, path, and target location of a binaurally recorded goalball throw?
  • How do throw type, speed, angle of approach, and listening position affect localization accuracy?
  • How early can experienced players, low-vision non-players, and sighted non-players identify the ball path?
  • How much do calibration, headphone playback, microphone setup, and HRTF matching influence realism and performance?
  • Can synthetic binaural rendering match actual binaural recordings closely enough for virtual play and localization training?
  • How does recording height, such as kneeling versus standing, change a listener's ability to recognize the line of approach?

Research note

Living Labs community research pilot.

Our current Living Labs pilot studies how support, communication, scheduling, and coordination work inside an independent living community. The research is designed to include multiple perspectives in the same environment so resident experience, staff workflow, and leadership priorities can be understood together.

Research intent

The pilot examines the everyday barriers people encounter when requesting, receiving, coordinating, and delivering support. This surfaces unmet needs that are specific enough to guide better solutions.

Research approach

The model combines resident, staff, and leadership perspectives through structured conversations and synthesis. This helps distinguish individual preferences from shared patterns across the community.

Why Living Labs matter

Living Labs make research more grounded because they study needs inside the real environments where solutions must work. That context helps reveal operational constraints, trust factors, and adoption requirements earlier.

Research questions.

The pilot is focused on understanding the full context around support needs before defining solution opportunities.

  • How do residents identify, communicate, and prioritize support needs?
  • Where do scheduling, communication, or coordination breakdowns create friction?
  • How do staff experience the same processes from an operational point of view?
  • What information does leadership need to improve service quality and evaluate new approaches?
  • Which needs appear isolated, and which patterns point to higher-impact opportunities?
