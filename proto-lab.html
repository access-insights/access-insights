UnisonOS

An open source operating surface for intention-centered computing.

UnisonOS exists to reduce the friction between what a person wants to accomplish and the digital work required to get there. Instead of asking people to navigate apps, files, windows, and workflows, UnisonOS treats intention as the primary interface and orchestrates services, agents, context, and modalities around the person.

Why it exists

Modern computing often forces people to manage tool complexity before they can act. UnisonOS explores a more humane model where systems respect time, reduce cognitive overhead, and adapt to the way each person communicates, senses, and works.

How it works

The platform uses an intent graph, orchestrator, policy and consent services, local-first inference, context services, storage, and real-time experience rendering to turn multimodal input into coordinated action.

What makes it different

Voice, text, vision, touch, Braille, sign, and BCI are treated as first-class channels. Accessibility is not a separate mode; the system generates experiences around a person's preferences, capabilities, device context, and consent settings.

What the open source project includes.

The public workspace is a multi-repo system with a developer front door, local devstack, shared libraries, documentation, and services for orchestration, context, storage, inference, policy, consent, authentication, I/O, rendering, communications, and actuation.

  • Edge-first architecture that can run locally, with cloud providers treated as optional and policy-controlled.
  • Privacy and consent primitives built into the control plane, including auditability for higher-impact actions.
  • Real-time generated experiences that present what is useful in the moment instead of relying on persistent navigation chrome.
  • Developer workflows for bootstrapping the workspace, starting the devstack, running smoke tests, and validating service compatibility.

Virtual Lex

Establishing presence when you cannot be present.

Virtual Lex began as a way for Lex Frieden to virtually attend a series of 35th anniversary ADA celebrations with a realistic sense of being in the room. The project exists because remote participation should be able to carry presence, directionality, audience connection, and agency when physical attendance is not possible.

Why it exists

Video calls flatten the room. Virtual Lex explores what becomes possible when remote presence preserves spatial hearing, body orientation, visible representation, and the ability to engage with people directly.

How it works

A physical torso uses glasses-mounted cameras and binaural microphones positioned at the ears. The torso can turn toward people asking questions so the remote participant can hear, see, and engage from a more realistic perspective.

Audience experience

People in the room experience Lex as an avatar with animation and lip-sync matched to his actual speech, creating a more embodied and interactive presence than a conventional remote feed.

  • High-precision binaural audio supports a realistic sense of where voices and room sounds are coming from.
  • Camera placement supports seeing the room from the perspective of the remote participant.
  • Physical orientation supports direct engagement with audience members during questions and discussion.
  • Avatar representation gives the audience a visible social presence to engage with in the room.
